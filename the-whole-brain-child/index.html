Total: 6 Chapters | 12 Strategies | 4 Types of Integration

The Whole-Brain Child bridges the gap between neuroscience and parenting. Drawing on the latest brain research, Siegel and Bryson explain how a child's brain is wired, how it matures, and how parents can turn everyday moments into opportunities to help the whole brain develop.

The central insight: Integration is the key to mental health. When the different parts of the brain work together—left with right, upstairs with downstairs—children thrive emotionally, intellectually, and socially.

"What you do matters. How you interact with your children when they're upset, when they're scared, when they act out—these are the moments that shape their brains."
— Daniel J. Siegel & Tina Payne Bryson

The 12 Whole-Brain Strategies at a Glance

1
Connect and Redirect

When emotions run high, connect right brain to right brain first, then redirect with the left.

2
Name It to Tame It

Help children tell the story of scary or painful experiences to calm big emotions.

3
Engage, Don't Enrage

Appeal to the upstairs brain instead of triggering the downstairs brain.

4
Use It or Lose It

Exercise the upstairs brain by giving children opportunities for complex thinking.

5
Move It or Lose It

Physical movement can shift emotional states and restore balance.

6
Use the Remote of the Mind

After overwhelming experiences, help children pause, rewind, and fast-forward through memories.

7
Remember to Remember

Help children develop memory by asking them to actively recall important experiences.

8
Let the Clouds of Emotion Roll By

Teach children that feelings come and go—they are temporary states, not permanent traits.

9
SIFT: Paying Attention to What's Inside

Help children explore Sensations, Images, Feelings, and Thoughts within themselves.

10
Exercise Mindsight

Help children understand their own minds and develop empathy for others.

11
Increase the Family Fun Factor

Build positive experiences that create joyful connections and model healthy relating.

12
Connect Through Conflict

Use conflicts as opportunities to teach empathy, perspective-taking, and repair.

Core Concepts of The Whole-Brain Child

  • Integration = Mental Health: When different parts of the brain work together, children are balanced, resilient, and able to handle life's challenges
  • The Developing Brain: Children's brains aren't finished—the upstairs brain isn't fully developed until the mid-20s
  • Connect First, Redirect Second: You can't reason with an upset child until you've connected emotionally
  • Moments of Chaos = Opportunities: Tantrums, meltdowns, and conflicts are chances to wire the brain for integration
  • Name It to Tame It: Putting feelings into words engages the left brain and calms the right brain's emotional flood
  • Mindsight: The ability to see your own mind and others' minds—the foundation of emotional intelligence and empathy
